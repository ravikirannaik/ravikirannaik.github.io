Introduction

This undergraduate course provides a comprehensive introduction to labor economics, examining how labor markets function, how wages are determined, and how institutions shape employment outcomes. The course draws on both theoretical frameworks and empirical evidence, with particular attention to labor market issues in developing economies.

Key questions addressed:

  • How are wages determined in competitive and non-competitive labor markets?
  • What role does human capital play in labor market outcomes?
  • How do institutions such as unions, minimum wages, and regulations affect employment?
  • What are the implications of informality, platform work, and technological change for labor markets?

Part 1: Labor Supply and Demand

This section covers the fundamental models of labor supply and demand, including the income-leisure tradeoff, labor demand under different market structures, and equilibrium in competitive labor markets.

Key Topics
  • Individual labor supply decisions
  • Firm labor demand
  • Competitive equilibrium
  • Monopsony and imperfect competition

Part 2: Human Capital and Wage Determination

This section examines human capital theory, returns to education, on-the-job training, and the determinants of wage differentials across workers and occupations.

Key Topics
  • Schooling and returns to education
  • On-the-job training
  • Education-occupation mismatch
  • Wage differentials and discrimination

Part 3: Labor Market Institutions and Contemporary Issues

This section covers labor market institutions, informality, platform work, and the future of work, drawing on evidence from India and other developing economies.

Key Topics
  • Labor market institutions and regulations
  • Informal employment
  • Platform/gig economy
  • AI, automation, and the future of work
